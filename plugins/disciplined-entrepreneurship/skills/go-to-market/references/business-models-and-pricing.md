# Business Models, GTM Motions & Pricing

Operational companion to DE Steps 15–18. The canonical 17 business-model archetypes live in
`disciplined-entrepreneurship/references/theme-4-how-you-make-money.md`; this file is about
**choosing** among them, picking a **GTM motion**, and **pricing**.

## Choosing a business model (Step 15)

Score candidate archetypes against four lenses (all derived from Steps 1–14):
1. **Value creation** — the quantified value (Step 8); the *timing and risk* of value delivery and the
   *unit* you charge on (per seat / usage / transaction / outcome). Charge on the unit that scales with
   the value the customer perceives.
2. **Customer factors** — the DMU and buying process; capital vs. operating budget preference;
   procurement constraints. Match the model to how the customer is *allowed* to buy.
3. **Competition** — how incumbents charge, and where a different model is itself a wedge (e.g.
   usage-based against a seat-based incumbent).
4. **Internal operations** — effect on sales friction, COCA, LTV, billing/support complexity, and your
   access to capital.

**Archetypes grouped by shape** (full list in the DE reference): recurring (subscription, usage-based,
"cell-phone" allotment), transaction (marketplace cut, payments), asset + ongoing (one-time +
maintenance, operating & maintenance, consumables, razor-and-blade), IP (licensing), attention/data
(advertising, data resale), outcome (shared-savings/gain-share), and system (franchise). Strategic
notes: recurring revenue is the biggest LTV lever; cost-plus caps your upside; "free" must have a
capture path; razor-and-blade and shared-savings align price with realized value.

## Choosing a GTM motion (Step 18)

| | Product-led (PLG / bottoms-up) | Sales-led (top-down) |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer | Individual user / team adopts | Economic buyer, committee |
| Fits | Low friction, short time-to-value, low ACV, high volume | High ACV, complex, multi-stakeholder, compliance-heavy |
| Acquisition | Self-serve, freemium, content, virality | Outbound, demos, pilots, RFPs |
| COCA | Low per customer, but needs scale | High per deal, demands high LTV |
| DMU fit | User ≈ champion ≈ buyer | Roles diverge; champion sells internally |
| Watch-outs | Monetizing free users; activation | Long cycles; cash burn before revenue |

**Hybrid / sales-assisted** blends them (e.g. PLG to land, sales to expand). The motion is the
dominant driver of COCA, so model it across the three horizons (short high-touch → long self-serve).
Keep it consistent with the DMU (Step 12) and the buying process (Step 13).

## Pricing playbook (Step 16)

- **Anchor on value, not cost or competitors.** Price as a fraction of the quantified Step 8 value;
  a common heuristic is to leave the customer ~4–5× the value they pay so the buy is an obvious win.
- **Segment** prices by customer type / willingness to pay within the market.
- **Lighthouse customers** — give early marquee references flexible *early* terms for validation,
  testimonials, and word of mouth (which lowers future COCA). Structure so it doesn't permanently
  anchor your price.
- **Direction of travel** — it's far easier to *drop* a price than raise it; never set a high public
  list price and then discount it publicly (it trains buyers to wait and signals weakness).
- **Iterate** — pricing is a first pass; revisit as you learn willingness to pay (which you only truly
  know once someone has paid or fake-paid).
- **Mind the budget** — capex vs. opex and the fiscal cycle affect both the model and the price the
  economic buyer can approve.

## Metrics that pair with this (post-launch)

North Star Metric (a single leading proxy for delivered value) + Pirate Metrics / AARRR (Acquisition,
Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue) instrument the funnel. They are not DE-native but pair with
LTV (Step 17), COCA (Step 19), and the ongoing "are the dogs still eating the dog food" check
(Step 23).
