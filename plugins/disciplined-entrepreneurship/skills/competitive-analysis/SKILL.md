# Competitive Analysis — Customer-Centric, Then Strategic

In Disciplined Entrepreneurship the competitive question is answered **through the customer's eyes**,
not by tallying features. What matters is how you rank on the **two priorities the persona cares about
most** (Step 11), and whether your **Core** (Step 10) makes you durably hard to beat. The toughest
competitor is usually the **status quo** — a spreadsheet, an internal hack, or doing nothing.

## The DE core method (do this first)

1. **Identify the real alternatives** — including "do nothing." List who/what the persona uses today
   to address their #1 priority. (See `../disciplined-entrepreneurship/references/theme-2-what-can-you-do.md`.)
2. **Define your Core (Step 10)** — the one thing you do better than anyone, hard to replicate, that
   compounds. Distinguish it from a *moat* (slows competitors but can be overcome) and from your
   product itself.
3. **Chart the competitive position (Step 11)** — a 2×2 whose axes are the persona's **top two
   purchasing criteria**. Plot every competitor *and the status quo*. You must land **upper-right**.
   If you don't, the finding is real signal: revisit your Core and your market selection — don't
   redraw the axes to flatter yourself.

This customer-priority 2×2 is the DE-native artifact. The frameworks below elaborate it.

## The broader toolkit (use to enrich, not replace)

Pick the lens that fits the question — full descriptions, when-to-use, and DE mapping in
`references/frameworks.md`:
- **Feature comparison matrix** — granular parity table for sales enablement and gap analysis.
- **2×2 positioning map** — communicate differentiation simply (this is Step 11).
- **Porter's Five Forces** — industry-structure profitability (rivalry, entrants, substitutes,
  supplier & buyer power). Sanity-check whether the beachhead sits in a profitable industry.
- **SWOT** — fast organizing checklist; only useful if each entry ties to evidence and an action.
- **Positioning (April Dunford, 5 components)** — competitive alternatives → unique attributes →
  value & proof → best-fit customers → market category. The GTM framing DE leaves implicit.
- **Blue Ocean strategy canvas** — plot competing factors to find uncontested space; pairs with
  choosing a differentiated Core.

## Researching competitors

Dispatch the **`competitor-analyst`** agent to research a competitor set in parallel: what each does
and for whom, pricing (with source + date), strengths/weaknesses from the customer's POV, review-site
sentiment (G2/Capterra/Reddit), and funding. It returns profiles + a comparison matrix.

## Output

Write one profile per competitor into the venture's `competitors/` folder, maintain
`competitors/matrix.md` (feature matrix + the 2×2), and record the position in
`02-what-can-you-do.md` (Step 11). Frame everything in **customer benefits**, not your features.
