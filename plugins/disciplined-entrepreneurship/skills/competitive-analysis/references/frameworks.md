# Competitive & Positioning Frameworks

For each: a tight description, when to use it, and how it maps to Disciplined Entrepreneurship. The
DE-native artifact is the **customer-priority 2×2 (Step 11)**; these elaborate or complement it.

## Competitive-analysis matrices

- **Feature comparison matrix** — competitors × the capabilities the persona cares about. Granular;
  best for sales enablement and gap analysis. Risk: devolves into a feature war detached from
  benefits. Keep rows tied to persona priorities.
- **2×2 positioning map** — two customer-relevant axes; best for communicating differentiation
  simply. **This is the Step 11 format** — axes = the persona's top two purchasing criteria; plot
  competitors *and "do nothing."*
- **Gartner Magic Quadrant style** — Completeness of Vision (x) vs. Ability to Execute (y). An
  analyst-evaluation lens for mature markets; useful to understand how enterprise buyers are told to
  think, less so for an early beachhead.
- **Blue Ocean strategy canvas (value curve)** — plot the industry's competing factors and your own
  curve to find uncontested space (raise/create some factors, reduce/eliminate others). Best when
  seeking value innovation; pairs naturally with choosing a differentiated **Core (Step 10)**.

## Porter's Five Forces (industry structure)

Industry attractiveness/profitability from: competitive rivalry, threat of new entrants, threat of
substitutes, supplier power, buyer power. **Use** for macro/structural profitability — *not*
customer-level positioning. **DE mapping:** supplements Step 11, but the lenses differ — DE's chart
is **end-user-priority-centric**; Five Forces is **industry-structure-centric**. Use it to sanity-check
whether the beachhead sits in a structurally profitable industry, not to position the product.

## SWOT

Strengths/Weaknesses (internal) vs. Opportunities/Threats (external). **Use** as a fast organizing
checklist. **Caveat:** descriptive, not prescriptive; easily hand-wavy and unranked — only useful when
each entry ties to evidence and a next action. **DE mapping:** no native step; S/W loosely inform the
**Core (Step 10)**, O/T feed Step 11 and **key assumptions (Step 20)**. DE's quantified steps
generally supersede it.

## Positioning — April Dunford, *Obviously Awesome*

Five components, worked in order:
1. **Competitive alternatives** — what customers would do without you (incl. the status quo).
2. **Unique attributes / capabilities** — what only you have.
3. **Value (and proof)** those attributes enable — the benefit, evidenced.
4. **Target-market characteristics** — who cares a lot, fast (your best-fit customers).
5. **Market category / frame of reference** — the context that makes your value obvious.
(+ optional **relevant trends**.)

**Use** at launch, after a pivot, when entering a new segment, or when sales stall because buyers
"don't get it." **DE mapping:** component 1 ↔ Step 11; component 4 ↔ Steps 2–5 (beachhead/profile/
persona); components 2–3 ↔ Steps 8–10. Dunford's **category choice** is the GTM framing DE leaves
implicit — decide it deliberately.

## Other lenses worth knowing

- **Jobs-to-be-Done (Christensen / Ulwick ODI)** — customers "hire" a product to make progress on a
  job (functional/emotional/social). Segment by job, not demographics. Feeds Step 6 (use case) and
  Step 8 (outcomes as value metrics); the "buying job" explains different DMU members (Step 12).
- **Value Proposition Canvas (Strategyzer)** — customer profile (jobs/pains/gains) vs. value map
  (products, pain relievers, gain creators); "fit" when they match. Operationalizes Steps 8–10. (It
  overlaps heavily with JTBD — pick one as primary to avoid duplication.)

## How to choose

- Positioning a product against alternatives for a customer → **Step 11 2×2** (+ Dunford for the
  category/messaging).
- Deciding whether the *industry* is worth entering → **Porter's Five Forces**.
- Finding white space / value innovation → **Blue Ocean strategy canvas**.
- Sales enablement / closing feature gaps → **feature matrix**.
- Quick internal stock-take → **SWOT** (then convert each item to evidence + action).
Always end customer-facing analysis in **benefits the persona ranks**, not your feature list.
