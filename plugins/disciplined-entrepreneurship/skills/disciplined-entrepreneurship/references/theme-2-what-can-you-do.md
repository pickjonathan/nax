# Theme 2 — What Can You Do for Your Customer? (Steps 7–11)

Theme 1 told you *who*. Theme 2 defines *what for them* — specified, quantified, and validated
against the persona's ranked priorities. Two steps look outward (7, 8, 9, 11); one looks inward (10).

---

## Step 7 — High-Level Product Specification

**Question:** What product, at a high level, will you build — described by the **benefits** it
delivers against the persona's ranked priorities? **Don't build it yet.** A brochure takes little
time, so you stay unattached and iterate cheaply (avoiding the "I built it so I love it" trap). Its
job is **team alignment** and **low-cost iteration with the persona** — not selling.

**Tool — two artifacts:**
- **(A) A visual of the product** — hardware: a labeled drawing; software: a **storyboard of
  simulated screenshots** mapped onto the Step 6 use case. **Benefits first, then features** —
  *"customers don't care about features, they care about benefits."*
- **(B) A one-page brochure / mock press release** for the persona: company + product name and
  tagline, product image, **the #1 benefit aligned to the persona's #1 priority ("don't be
  subtle")**, two more benefits for priorities #2 and #3, a **quantified sense of magnitude** (from
  Step 8, e.g. "50% faster"), and a clear **call to action**.

**How to do it:** build the visual → map each headline benefit to the persona's #1/#2/#3 criteria →
draft the brochure → **iterate with the persona** and revise → keep it high-level and benefits-first.
Do not let non-beachhead customers influence the spec.

**Common mistakes:** building/prototyping too early; **feature wars** instead of benefits tied to the
#1 priority; over-technical specs; being "subtle" about the #1 benefit; ignoring persona feedback.

---

## Step 8 — Quantify the Value Proposition

**Question:** How much value — in the customer's **own quantified units** — does the product create
for the persona's **#1 priority**? Express value in the persona's language (dollars/year if they care
about cost; downtime/failure rate if they care about reliability).

**Tool — the Quantified Value Proposition (as-is vs. possible state).** One worksheet split
horizontally: **top = "as-is"** (their current process for the #1 priority, broken into steps, each
annotated with its cost in the chosen unit); **bottom = "possible"** (the same steps *with your
product*, in the *identical* units). **The gap between the halves is the quantified value.**

**Method:**
> **Quantified value = (as-is cost) − (possible-state cost), in the persona's #1-priority units**, per
> period or per cycle. E.g. "Today: 5 hours and $500 per cycle. With us: 1 hour and $100." → value =
> 4 hours + $400 saved per cycle.

**How to do it:** pull the persona's #1 priority → establish the **measurement unit** with the persona
→ map the **as-is** state (confirm numbers and terminology back with them) → define the **possible**
state in the same units → compute the **gap** → **validate the whole thing with the persona**.

**Rule — underpromise and over-deliver.** Danger signal: *"if the persona is not excited by your
conservative value proposition and is only excited by a more aggressive set of promises, that should
signal danger."*

**Common mistakes:** overpromising; expressing value in *your* units (features) not the customer's;
quantifying against several priorities at once instead of the #1; inventing numbers instead of
PMR-grounding them.

---

## Step 9 — Identify Your Next 10 Customers

**Question:** Beyond your one persona, can you name ~10 real potential customers who fit the
profile + persona — and do they actually want and will they buy this? This injects "a huge dose of
reality" and validates the beachhead is a **real, homogeneous market**, not a market of one.

**Tool — four worksheets:**
- **Contact list** — list **more than 10** names (to absorb non-responses); per contact: contact
  info, **source** (how you found them), and **how closely they match** the persona/profile.
- **Conversation notes** — one per conversation: demographics, psychographics, overall fit, does the
  Step 6 use case hold, does the Step 8 value prop resonate, and the **soft-commit question** ("If a
  company offered this, would you commit to purchase it?").
- **Summary of the 10** — side by side, to confirm **(a) homogeneity** (all the same market) and
  **(b) strong purchase interest**.
- **Lessons learned** — list **hypotheses** before outreach; record the **verdict** on each after
  (validated / invalidated / modify).

**Decision gate:** if consistency or interest is weak, **loop back** and reassess fundamentals. *"Don't
delude yourself — be intellectually honest."* The persona is mutable: *"There is nothing wrong with
changing your persona."* The signal you want is **love, not just like.**

**Common mistakes:** settling for the first 10 *available* rather than 10 *homogeneous-fit* contacts;
talking to economic buyers/influencers instead of end users; pretending a heterogeneous market is
homogeneous; not looping back when the signal is weak.

## Step 10 — Define Your Core

**Question:** What is the **one** thing you do better than anyone, that is **very hard to replicate**
and **gets stronger over time**? The first inward-facing step. *"Making sales without a Core is not
sustainable — your success will only draw attention to the opportunity, and competitors will rush
in."*

**Three criteria a true Core must meet:**
1. **Unique** — hard for anyone to replicate.
2. **Important** — tied directly to producing something the customer values highly (links to Step 8).
3. **Grows** — strengthens over time relative to competitors.

**Core vs. moat:** a *moat* slows competitors but can be overcome; a *Core* keeps you ahead. Moats buy
time to strengthen the Core; they don't replace it.

**Candidate types of Core:** network effects; lowest-cost / economies of scale; locked-in customer
relationships & loyalty; **switching costs**; superior service; UX/design; **intellectual property**;
brand; a locked-up network of partners/suppliers/distribution. **Two things that are NOT a Core:**
first-mover advantage, and the product itself (the hard-to-copy capability *beneath* the product is
the Core).

**How to do it:** inventory all assets → rank by relative strength → test each against the 3 criteria
to separate Cores from moats → **select exactly ONE Core** (don't diffuse focus) → be able to justify
the choice. Changing your Core later is very costly, so choose deliberately.

**SensAble example:** the Core was redefined away from the obvious-but-copyable hardware + patents
toward **the physics/algorithms of 3-D touch (the haptics software)** — the compounding,
value-tied, hard-to-replicate capability. The hardware was merely a moat.

**Common mistakes:** confusing a **moat for a Core** (patents, first-mover, one matchable feature);
picking more than one Core; choosing a non-growing advantage; flip-flopping later.

---

## Step 11 — Chart Your Competitive Position

**Question:** On the persona's **top two priorities**, do customers see you as clearly the best
option — better than competitors **and** better than "do nothing"? The outward-facing counterpart to
Step 10. It validates that Steps 1–10 produce a *defensible, customer-perceived* position.

**Tool — the Competitive Positioning Chart (a 2×2 matrix).** **The axes are the persona's top two
purchasing criteria** (from Step 5): X = priority #1, Y = priority #2. You belong **upper-right**
(strong on both). **Plot every alternative on the same axes — all major competitors *and the
"do-nothing"/status-quo option*** (often the toughest competitor). This is about the customer's
priorities, not your features. Step 11 is where you **translate your Core (Step 10) into customer-perceived
value** on the axes the customer actually ranks.

**How to do it:** make the persona's top-2 priorities the axes → plot all competitors **plus "do
nothing"** → plot yourself based on your Core + PMR → **if you're not upper-right, go back and revisit
your Core (10) and market selection (1–5).**

**SensAble example:** axes = the designer's top priorities (fidelity of working naturally in 3-D;
speed of the design cycle); SensAble plots upper-right; hand-sculpting clay and 2-D CAD fall short;
"do nothing" anchors the low region.

**Common mistakes:** analyzing competitor *features* instead of customer *benefits*; **omitting the
"do nothing" alternative**; inventing axes not grounded in PMR; placing yourself upper-right by
wishful thinking rather than evidence.

---

## Step 10 vs. Step 11 — the distinction

| | **Step 10 — Core** | **Step 11 — Competitive Position** |
|---|---|---|
| **Direction** | **Inward** — look at yourself | **Outward** — look through the customer's eyes |
| **Question** | What are we uniquely best at, hard to copy, compounding? | On the customer's top-2 priorities, are we the obvious best vs. competitors *and* "do nothing"? |
| **Frame** | Your assets/capabilities (the engine) | The customer's prioritized benefits (the scoreboard) |
| **Durability** | Must compound over time (a Core, not a moat) | A snapshot of *perceived* position today |
| **Drives** | Step 11, pricing, defensibility | Validation of Steps 1–10; investor & customer messaging |

The **Core is internal and durable**; the **Competitive Position is external and customer-facing**.
Land outside the upper-right → revisit the Core (10) and/or market selection (1–5).

## How Theme 2 hands off to Theme 3

You now have a specified product, a quantified value proposition, a validated homogeneous market, a
chosen Core, and a defensible competitive position. Theme 3 asks **who decides to buy and how** — the
DMU and the buying process.
