# DE Overview — Philosophy, Flow, and How the Steps Interlock

Read this once to hold the whole framework in your head. Then load the specific
`theme-N-*.md` file before guiding a step.

## The axiom that governs everything

> **A paying customer is the single necessary and sufficient condition for a business.**
> The day someone pays you money for your product, you have a business — and not a day before.

A business is not a product, a technology, or a passion. *"If nobody buys your product, you
simply do not have a business."* Corollary founders forget: **the customer is not paying you to
exist.** They pay for value delivered to *them*, measured in *their* terms. So you **begin with
the customer and work everything back from there.**

## Why 24 steps, why this order

The 24 steps form a logical chain where **each step's output is the next step's input.** They
answer six questions in sequence:

1. **Who is your customer?** (1–6) — Start wide (every possible market), narrow to **one**
   battlefield, zoom to the typical user, size it, collapse to **one named real person**, and map
   their full lived experience with the product.
2. **What can you do for your customer?** (7–11) — Specify the product against that person's
   ranked priorities, **quantify** the value, prove it's a real market (not a market of one),
   define the durable thing only you can do (Core), and show you win on the customer's priorities.
3. **How does your customer acquire your product?** (12–13) — Who is in the decision, and what is
   the real buying process.
4. **How do you make money off your product?** (14–19) — The size of the bigger prize, the
   business model, pricing, and the unit economics (LTV vs COCA).
5. **How do you design & build your product?** (20–23) — Isolate the riskiest assumptions, test
   them cheaply, build the minimum viable *business* product, and prove customers use and pay.
6. **How do you scale your business?** (24) — The product plan and follow-on sequencing.

> **Note on the Step-14 boundary.** Step 14 (TAM for Follow-on Markets) sits at the seam between
> "acquire" and "make money." This toolkit places it at the head of Theme 4 because it sizes the
> revenue opportunity beyond the beachhead; some reproductions show it closing Theme 3. The content
> is identical either way.

## The single-beachhead doctrine (the reason for the funnel)

> *"A key determinant of success is the ability to select a market and to stay disciplined by
> deselecting other markets."*

Three reinforcing metaphors:
- **D-Day / Normandy beachhead** — take and hold one small defensible shore with concentrated
  force, then push inland. Spread thin across the whole coast and you are weak everywhere.
- **Bowling-pin strategy** (Geoffrey Moore) — the beachhead is the head pin; knocking it down
  topples adjacent pins (follow-on markets) via reusable product, references, and word of mouth.
- **Market-share dominance** — pick a market small enough to dominate *quickly*. Dominance yields
  pricing power, reference customers, word of mouth, cash flow, and a base to fund expansion.

First-time founders think more markets = better odds. The opposite is true: it *"will decrease
your odds of success, because you and your new enterprise will lack the necessary focus."*

## The method threaded through every step: Primary Market Research (PMR)

Every step is answered with evidence from **direct contact with real potential customers**, in
**inquiry, not advocacy** mode — you listen and learn, you never pitch or sell. *"The customer is
the expert of their problem; you are the expert in finding a solution."* Primary beats secondary:
*"There are no customers in Frost & Sullivan reports,"* and *"if there is already a market research
report with all the information you need, it is probably too late for your new venture."* Early on
you are *"a data point of one,"* so stay skeptical of your own conclusions. **Never stop doing PMR
— the day you stop is the day your company starts to die.** (Full method: `primary-market-research.md`.)

## Validation is iterative, not linear

The steps build logically, but real validation **loops back constantly**. An interview at Step 9
routinely rewrites the persona from Step 5. Failing the upper-right test at Step 11 sends you back
to your Core (10) and market selection (1–5). Treat the venture workspace as a living document and
revise earlier steps without shame — *"there is nothing wrong with changing your Persona."* Intellectual
honesty is the core virtue: *"Don't delude yourself."*

## How facts and assumptions are handled

At every step, separate what you **know** (evidenced by a customer or a source) from what you
**believe** (an assumption). Tag each. Route assumptions to `assumptions.md` so they can be ranked
(Step 20) and tested (Step 21). A plan built on unlabelled assumptions is a plan that fails silently.

## The running example used across the references: SensAble Technologies

Aulet's signature case (his own company). **FreeForm** is a force-feedback *haptic* ("digital
touch") 3D-modeling system for industrial/product designers who sculpt organic shapes (toys,
jewelry, footwear). It recurs through Steps 1, 4, 5, 7, 10, and 11 so the example compounds the way
the steps do. Watch how a single haptic technology fans into ~10 candidate segments (Step 1),
narrows to industrial-design sculpting (Step 2), and sizes to a deliberately small ~$10–15M
beachhead TAM (Step 4).

## Editions

Standardize on the **2nd edition (2024)** numbering: MVBP = Step 22, "dogs eat the dog food" =
Step 23, Product Plan = Step 24; LTV = 17, COCA = 19. The 2nd edition retitled Step 18 to "Design a
Scalable Revenue Engine" (same content). Formulas (TAM, LTV, COCA) are stable across editions.
The companion book, *Disciplined Entrepreneurship: Startup Tactics* (Paul Cheek & Bill Aulet, 2024),
covers **execution** (15 tactics in 4 stages) once these 24 strategy steps produce a validated plan.
