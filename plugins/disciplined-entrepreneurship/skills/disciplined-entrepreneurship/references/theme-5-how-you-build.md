# Theme 5 — How Do You Design & Build Your Product? (Steps 20–23)

You have a strategy. Before betting the company on it, isolate the riskiest beliefs, test them
cheaply, build the smallest thing that proves customers will **use and pay**, and gather real
evidence. This is empirical de-risking — the bridge from plan to product.

---

## Step 20 — Identify Key Assumptions

**Question:** What unproven "leap-of-faith" beliefs is the whole plan resting on? You've made dozens
of assumptions across Steps 1–19; isolate the few that, if wrong, sink the venture.

**Tool — a ranked list of 5–10 key assumptions**, distilled from the prior steps and **prioritized by
importance × uncertainty**. Typical examples: price acceptance, sales-cycle length, churn/retention,
a critical technical feasibility, a supplier cost, the word-of-mouth/virality rate.

**How to do it:** walk back through Steps 1–19 → extract every load-bearing assumption → rank them →
single out the ones that are cheap to test but catastrophic if false. Record them in
`assumptions.md`.

**Common mistakes:** confusing *facts* with *assumptions*; listing trivial assumptions; failing to
prioritize the truly business-killing ones first.

---

## Step 21 — Test Key Assumptions

**Question:** Can you cheaply prove each key assumption true *before* building the full product?
Empirical de-risking ahead of the build.

**Method — design the minimal experiment per assumption** (fast, cheap, real-world):
- **Pre-orders / letters of intent / landing pages** → test price and demand.
- **Supplier quotes** → test cost assumptions.
- **Prototypes / mock-ups** → test feature value and feasibility.
- **Direct customer conversations and small live tests** → test behavior.

Prefer **behavioral evidence over opinion** — what people *do* (sign up, pre-pay, commit time), not
what they *say*. This is the Build–Measure–Learn loop from Lean Startup applied to the riskiest
assumptions first.

**How to do it:** for each ranked assumption, define the cheapest test that would change your mind →
state the success threshold *before* running it (avoid confirmation bias) → run it → record
validated/invalidated in `assumptions.md`. Invalidated key assumptions send you back to the relevant
earlier step (pivot or persevere).

**Common mistakes:** relying on surveys/opinions instead of behavior; over-building to test (the test
should be lighter than the product); testing low-priority assumptions first; moving the success
criteria after seeing results.

---

## Step 22 — Define the Minimum Viable Business Product (MVBP)

**Question:** What is the smallest thing you can put in front of customers that proves they get value
**and will pay**? This is where the MVBP **diverges sharply from a generic MVP**.

**The three required elements — all must be present:**
1. **The customer gets value** from *using* it (it solves the core problem) — *delivers value.*
2. **The customer pays** for it (real money or a strong commitment) — *captures value.*
3. **It starts the customer feedback loop**, so customers help you iterate — *creates learning.*

**MVBP vs generic MVP:** a Lean-Startup MVP typically only tests whether users *engage*. The MVBP also
demands **value capture (the customer pays)** and **a feedback loop**. Validation requires "a small
but real **business**," not a prototype people merely *like*. Build **only the core features**; resist
scope creep. For **two-sided markets**, the MVBP must show *both* sides working (engaged free users
*and* satisfied paying customers).

**Common mistakes:** building feature-rich instead of minimum; an MVP that proves usage but not
willingness to pay; no instrumentation to learn from; confusing a demo/prototype with an MVBP.

---

## Step 23 — Show That "The Dogs Will Eat the Dog Food"

**Question:** Will real customers actually use and pay — proven with **data, not opinions**? It doesn't
matter how good the ingredients are if the dogs won't eat it. This is real-market proof of
product-market fit.

**Evidence that counts (behavioral, quantitative):**
- **Actual purchases / paid subscriptions** — money changing hands.
- **Real usage & engagement** — frequency, depth, feature adoption.
- **Retention** — customers coming back; low churn.
- **Word-of-mouth / referral metrics** — develop explicit metrics for the level of word of mouth the
  MVBP generates (a leading indicator of falling COCA).

**Evidence that does NOT count:** surveys, "would you buy this?" intent, focus-group enthusiasm, free
signups with no usage.

**How to do it:** put the MVBP (Step 22) in real customers' hands → instrument usage, payment, and
referral → measure against thresholds → the result is quantitative proof of fit, or the signal to
pivot.

**Common mistakes:** mistaking interest/sign-ups for adoption; relying on surveys; cherry-picking
anecdotes instead of cohort metrics; not measuring word of mouth.
