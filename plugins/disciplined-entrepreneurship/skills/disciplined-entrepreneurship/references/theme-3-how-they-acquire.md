# Theme 3 — How Does Your Customer Acquire Your Product? (Steps 12–13)

You know who the customer is and what you do for them. Now map **who decides** and **how a purchase
actually happens** — the foundation for the sales process (Step 18) and COCA (Step 19).

---

## Step 12 — Determine the Customer's Decision-Making Unit (DMU)

**Question:** Who, specifically, is involved in the decision to buy — and what does each person care
about? In B2B (and often B2C) the user, the payer, and the advocate are rarely the same person. You
cannot design a sales process until you know every player.

**Tool — a DMU map** built for **real, named people**, not "general organizations." Even when one
person fills several roles, build a **separate profile per role** — the same person has different
priorities as user vs. buyer vs. champion.

**The roles, defined precisely:**
- **Champion** — the person who *wants the purchase made*; your internal advocate who pushes the deal
  through. Often (not always) the end user.
- **End User** — the person who will *actually use* the product. Their satisfaction drives renewal and
  word of mouth.
- **Primary Economic Buyer** — the person who *controls the money* and can sign off. *"Most important
  decisions are made by people with responsibility for the P&L."* Often holds implicit veto power.
- **Primary Influencers** — deep subject-matter experts whose opinion carries weight (a lead engineer,
  a respected peer). Influencers need not be people — a review or *Consumer Reports* counts.
- **Secondary Influencers** — lower-weight voices (other staff, analysts, media).
- **People with Veto Power** — anyone who can *kill* the purchase: senior management, IT, regulators,
  unions, an HOA, or policy. Veto holders are "particularly dangerous" — surface them early.
- **Purchasing Department** — usually holds **veto power only** and exists to *drive the price down*.
  **Neutralize them; do not try to sell to them.** They are rarely the user, buyer, or champion.

**How to do it:** from PMR, identify the real people in each role for your persona's organization →
profile each role's priorities, motivations, and influence → map relationships between them → flag
veto holders and the economic buyer explicitly.

**Common mistakes:** treating the DMU as a faceless company; assuming the user is the buyer; ignoring
veto holders until they kill the deal late; selling to purchasing instead of to the economic
buyer/champion.

## Step 13 — Map the Process to Acquire a Paying Customer

**Question:** What is the full sequence — and what hidden obstacles exist — between a prospect first
hearing of you and **money in your account**? This reveals **sales-cycle length**, surfaces
showstoppers (budget cycles, procurement authority, security/regulatory review), and is the raw
material for COCA (Step 19).

> Distinguish this (the customer's **buying** process, their point of view) from Step 18 (your
> **selling** process, your point of view).

**Tool — a process-flow map** across ~5 stages, tracking which DMU members are involved and how long
each takes:
1. **Generate awareness / lead** (channels, demand generation).
2. **Inform / evaluate** (demos, analysis, who else weighs in).
3. **Acquire** (proposal, negotiation, close).
4. **Install / onboard** (deployment, setup).
5. **Pay** (invoicing, terms, who cuts the check).

**How to do it:** for each stage, document who is involved, what they need, how long it takes, and
what could stall it → distinguish B2C vs B2B dynamics → estimate total **sales-cycle length** →
explicitly list budget authority, timing, and bureaucratic/regulatory obstacles.

**Common mistakes:** underestimating cycle length; missing the budgeting/procurement reality ("who can
actually pay, and when"); confusing the buying process (this step) with the selling process (Step 18).
