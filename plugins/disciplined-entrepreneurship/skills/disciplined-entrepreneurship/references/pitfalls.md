# Pitfalls — The Common Ways Founders Fool Themselves

A fast checklist of the highest-frequency, highest-damage mistakes, by theme. Use it as a review lens
on any venture — and as ammunition for the `assumption-red-teamer` agent.

## Cross-cutting traps (the big ones)

- **Falling in love with the solution, not the problem.** The customer isn't paying you to exist.
- **Skipping primary research** and reasoning from your own head or from analyst reports.
- **Advocacy instead of inquiry** in customer conversations — pitching contaminates the data.
- **Keeping multiple markets open** "to hedge." This *reduces* odds of success — focus is the point.
- **Unlabelled assumptions.** Treating beliefs as facts; a plan that fails silently.
- **Vanity validation.** Compliments, sign-ups, and survey enthusiasm mistaken for demand. Only
  commitment of time, reputation, or money counts.
- **Quantifying in your units (features), not the customer's** (dollars, hours, risk).

## Theme 1 — Who is your customer?

- Segmenting by **organization** instead of the **end user** (who actually uses it).
- Skipping the wide brainstorm and committing to one market too early.
- A profile/persona that is **fictional or stereotyped** rather than built from real observation.
- A market defined so broadly the **TAM balloons past $1B** (a sign segmentation was skipped).
- **Top-down-only TAM**; guessing revenue-per-user instead of grounding it in PMR.
- **Unranked** persona purchasing criteria, or criteria ranked by opinion.
- Mapping only the "use" stage of the life cycle, ignoring procurement, install, and support friction.

## Theme 2 — What can you do for your customer?

- **Building too early** (prototype attachment) instead of iterating on a cheap spec/brochure.
- **Feature wars** instead of benefits tied to the persona's #1 priority.
- **Overpromising** value — the danger signal is a persona only excited by aggressive promises.
- Pretending a **heterogeneous market is homogeneous** (Step 9) and not looping back.
- Confusing a **moat for a Core** (patents, first-mover, one copyable feature); picking >1 Core.
- Charting competition on **features**, omitting the **"do nothing"** alternative, or placing yourself
  upper-right by wishful thinking.

## Theme 3 — How does your customer acquire your product?

- Treating the DMU as a **faceless company**; assuming the **user is the buyer**.
- Ignoring **veto holders** (IT, legal, procurement) until they kill the deal late.
- **Selling to the purchasing department** instead of neutralizing them and selling to the economic
  buyer/champion.
- Underestimating **sales-cycle length** and the budget/procurement reality.

## Theme 4 — How do you make money?

- Defaulting to **"how the industry prices"**; choosing **cost-plus** and capping your upside.
- Treating **pricing as the business model**; relying on "free" with no capture mechanism.
- Setting a **high public price then discounting publicly** (trains the market to wait).
- Computing **LTV off revenue** instead of discounted gross profit; assuming 100% retention; ignoring
  cost of capital.
- The **COCA undercount**: "marketing ÷ customers," valuing founder selling time at zero, dividing by
  *total* not *new* customers.
- Comparing COCA to **revenue** instead of **LTV**; ignoring that healthy ventures need **LTV ≥ 3×COCA**.

## Theme 5 — How do you design & build?

- Confusing **facts with assumptions**; not ranking the business-killing ones first.
- Testing with **surveys/opinions** instead of behavioral experiments; moving success criteria after
  seeing results.
- An **MVP that proves usage but not willingness to pay** — not a true MVBP.
- Mistaking **interest/sign-ups for adoption**; cherry-picking anecdotes instead of cohort metrics.

## Theme 6 — How do you scale?

- **Abandoning the beachhead too early** to chase expansion.
- Treating the product plan as **fixed** rather than iterative.
- **Expanding before the core product is loved**; skipping the mini-24-Steps for each new market.
