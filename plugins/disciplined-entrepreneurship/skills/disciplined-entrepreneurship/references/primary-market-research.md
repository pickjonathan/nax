# Primary Market Research (PMR) — The DE Way

PMR is the fuel for all 24 steps. *"Never stop doing PMR — the day you stop is the day your company
starts to die."* This reference is the method; the `market-research` skill and the
`customer-discovery-analyst` agent operationalize it.

## Primary vs. secondary

- **Primary** = direct, first-hand interaction with potential customers: interviews, observation,
  immersion. Specific to *your* end user. Irreplaceable.
- **Secondary** = data someone else gathered (analyst reports, articles): useful for sizing and
  context, but filtered, often stale, and never about your specific customer. *"There are no customers
  in Frost & Sullivan reports."* If a report already has everything you need, you're probably too late.

DE insists on primary because your most important early customers almost always differ from who you
assumed, and the nuance that drives product/value/pricing decisions only surfaces through direct
contact (Steve Blank: *"There are no facts inside your building — get out of the building."*).

## The mindset: inquiry, not advocacy

- **Inquiry, not advocacy.** You are there to *understand the problem*, not to pitch, sell, or
  validate your ego. The moment you start explaining your solution, you contaminate the data.
- **Act like a great journalist** — active listening, empathy, intense curiosity. Listen ~80% of the
  time.
- **Seek first to understand, then to be understood.** Suppress the urge to describe your idea until
  you've grasped theirs.
- *"The customer is the expert of their problem; you are the expert in finding a solution."*
- **Three techniques:** interviews, **observation** (watch them work — actions beat words), and
  **immersion** (do their job yourself).

## The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick) — ask questions even your mom can't lie through

**Three rules:**
1. Talk about **their life**, not your idea.
2. Ask about **specifics in the past**, not generics or hypotheticals about the future.
3. **Talk less, listen more.**

**Three types of bad data to reject:**
- **Compliments** ("great idea!") — worthless ego-candy.
- **Fluff** (generics, hypotheticals, futures — "I usually…", "I would…").
- **Ideas / wish-lists** — chase the underlying problem, not the feature request.

**Commitment & advancement** — interest is only real when the customer gives up **currency**: *time*
(a real next meeting, a trial), *reputation* (intros to their boss/peers, a testimonial), or *money*
(an LOI, deposit, pre-order). "Yes, I'd buy it" with no commitment is noise.

## Interview question bank (steal these)

1. "Walk me through a typical day/week as it relates to [problem area]."
2. "Talk me through the **last time** [the problem] happened."
3. "How do you **currently** solve this today?"
4. "What tools or workarounds have you tried? What did you like / hate about them?"
5. "What's the **hardest part** about doing [task]?"
6. "Why is that hard? Why do you bother doing it at all?"
7. "What happens — what are the implications — when it goes wrong?"
8. "How **much does that problem cost** you — time, money, headcount?"
9. "How often does this come up — daily, weekly, monthly?"
10. "When was the last time you tried to fix this, and what did you do?"
11. "Who else is affected? Who actually makes the buying decision?"
12. "What are your big priorities right now?" (Is this even a priority?)
13. "Where does the budget for this come from today?"
14. "Anything I should have asked but didn't?"
15. "Who else should I talk to?" (advancement + referral)

## Anti-patterns — never ask these

1. "Do you think this is a good idea?" — opinion + flattery; only your mom says yes.
2. "Would you buy a product that did X?" — hypothetical; optimistic strangers say yes to be nice.
3. "How much would you pay for X?" — imagined future spending; people guess high, pay nothing.
4. "Would you pay $50/month?" — leading; anchors your desired number.
5. "You'd use this, right?" — fishing for a yes.
6. "Don't you hate it when [problem] happens?" — plants the problem and the emotion.
7. "How often do you *usually*…?" — "usually" invites fluff; demand the last specific instance.
8. "If we built [feature], would that help?" — hypothetical + pitches your roadmap.
9. "What features would you want?" — generates a wish-list; users are poor solution designers.
10. "Let me explain what we're building — what do you think?" — advocacy mode contaminates everything.

## Avoiding bias (Aulet's named pitfalls)

- **Confirmation bias** — set success criteria *before* the conversation; don't change metrics
  mid-stream; avoid leading questions.
- **Selection bias** — define the segment tightly. "Women" yields garbage; "freelance wedding
  photographers in the US" is researchable.
- **Social-acceptability bias** — don't interview friends/family; they won't risk offending you.
- Use **multiple interviewers** and a **second observer** to capture body language and de-bias the
  analysis. Early on you are *"a data point of one"* — stay skeptical of your own conclusions.

## How many, and synthesizing

Aim for ~**10–15 conversations per segment** before drawing patterns (Step 9 formalizes "the next 10
customers"). Then synthesize: recurring pains, the job-to-be-done, current workarounds and their
cost, the buying process, and surprises that contradicted your assumptions. Feed patterns into the
End User Profile (Step 3), Persona (Step 5), and `assumptions.md`. Log every conversation in the
venture's `interviews/` folder.
